Nursing Pathways

NMBA Guidelines — Effective 2 September 2025
NP

Nurse Practitioner

Can prescribe AND administer Schedule 4 cosmetic injectables within scope.

  • In-person or video consult required before each prescription
  • Professional indemnity insurance mandatory
  • CPD must include cosmetic-specific activities
Note: Working solely in cosmetics is unlikely to satisfy NMBA advanced practice requirements for NP endorsement.
RN

Registered Nurse

Can administer under a valid prescription. Cannot prescribe independently.

  • Min. 1-year post-registration nursing experience required first
  • Must work under an authorised prescriber arrangement
  • Responsible for supervising ENs in the workplace
RNs with sole mental health, paediatric, or disability qualifications must first complete general registration.
EN

Enrolled Nurse

Can work in this field with specific restrictions — must always be under RN supervision.

  • Min. 1-year post-registration EN experience required
  • Plus 1-year in a related area (e.g. dermatology)
  • Cannot inject very high-risk areas (glabella, nose, forehead)
  • GP supervision does NOT satisfy NMBA requirement

Doctor Pathways

Medical Board of Australia Guidelines Apply

What You Can Do

  • Prescribe and administer all Schedule 4 cosmetic injectables
  • Work independently or own a clinic from day one
  • Provide prescriber oversight for nurses in your clinic
  • Full range of treatment areas subject to competency

Key Obligations

  • Must train and work within your scope of competency
  • Professional indemnity must specifically cover cosmetic procedures
  • TGA compliance for all products used and advertised
  • Informed consent and patient assessment are mandatory

Dentist Pathways

Dental Board of Australia Applies

Unique Strengths

  • Deep knowledge of facial anatomy, nerves, and vascular structures
  • Masseter/TMJ treatments within dental scope — very high demand
  • Natural cross-referral from existing patient relationships
  • Can expand lower-face aesthetics within existing dental practice

Regulatory Requirements

  • Current AHPRA registration with Dental Board required
  • Must be within your scope of practice and clinical competence
  • State/territory drugs and poisons legislation applies
  • Indemnity insurance must specifically cover cosmetic procedures

      For Registered Nurses

      RN Job Opportunities

      RNs are among the most sought-after practitioners in cosmetic clinics across Australia.

      01
      Employed Injector

      The most common starting point for RNs entering the cosmetic space. You work within an established clinic under a prescriber arrangement, focusing entirely on building clinical skill and client relationships without the overhead of running a business. Most clinics offer structured mentorship, product training, and a ready-made client base — making this the lowest-risk, highest-support way to enter the industry.

      Entry LevelTraining SupportEstablished Path
      02
      Mobile / Contractor

      Once you've built solid clinical foundations, contracting gives you the freedom to work across multiple clinics on your own schedule. You'll need your own prescriber arrangement, professional indemnity insurance, and the discipline to manage your own bookings and income. In return, you gain exposure to a broader range of clients, techniques, and clinic cultures — and typically earn more per hour than an employed injector.

      FlexibleMultiple ClinicsIndependent
      03
      Cosmetic Nurse → Business Owner

      Many RNs who enter this industry with a clear business mindset transition to clinic ownership within 1–2 years — faster than almost any other healthcare pathway. Understanding compliance requirements, prescriber arrangements, and client acquisition from the start puts you miles ahead. The practitioners who succeed here treat their employed phase as a paid apprenticeship, not just a job, and arrive at ownership fully prepared rather than figuring it out on the fly.

      STEP 1

      Understand Your Practice Pathway

      Foundation Step
      What This Involves
      • Identify your registration type (RN, NP, EN, Doctor, Dentist) and understand the scope implications for injecting, prescribing, and clinic ownership
      • Research minimum experience requirements for cosmetic injectables specific to your profession and state or territory
      • Complete AHPRA-approved cosmetic injectable training — Foundation and Advanced modules from a recognised provider
      • Build supervised clinical experience over 6–12+ months under an experienced cosmetic injector before practising independently
      • Understand your scope of practice limitations and what procedures are legally permissible for your role
      Key Considerations
      • Doctors and Nurse Practitioners have the most direct and flexible pathways to independent practice and clinic ownership
      • RNs need a delegating prescriber and substantial supervised experience before practising independently
      • ENs face the most restrictions and typically cannot own or operate independently without significant oversight
      • State and territory requirements may impose additional obligations beyond AHPRA registration standards
      • Training quality varies — always choose providers recognised by ASAM, ACAM, or AIAM
      Pathway varies by profession: Doctors and NPs progress most quickly; RNs typically need 12+ months; ENs require ongoing supervision and generally cannot operate independently.
      STEP 2

      Establish Your Prescriber Model

      Required
      What This Involves
      • Confirm whether you require a prescribing arrangement — RNs and ENs always do; NPs and Doctors can prescribe within their own scope
      • Find a qualified Medical Practitioner or Nurse Practitioner willing to act as your prescribing practitioner
      • Establish a formal, written prescriber agreement detailing scope, responsibilities, and clinical oversight arrangements
      • Understand medication storage, cold-chain requirements, and documentation procedures for Schedule 4 medicines
      • Ensure the prescriber is accessible for clinical consultation, patient review, and emergency support when required
      Key Considerations
      • All prescriber agreements must be legally documented and reviewed regularly — verbal arrangements are non-compliant
      • The prescribing practitioner must be genuinely involved in patient care, not merely a name on paperwork
      • Prescriber fees vary — budget approximately $500 to $2,000+ per month depending on arrangement structure and volume
      • Telehealth prescribing has specific compliance requirements — the prescriber must conduct an appropriate patient assessment
      • Some state health authorities require prescriber arrangements to be formally registered — check your jurisdiction
      Mandatory for RNs and ENs. Without a valid prescriber arrangement, you cannot legally administer prescription cosmetic injectables regardless of training or experience level.
      STEP 3

      Facility & Compliance Setup

      Required
      What This Involves
      • Source or fit out clinical premises meeting treatment room standards — appropriate size, ventilation, waste disposal, and patient privacy
      • Register your facility with the state health authority if required (QLD, NSW, and VIC have the most detailed requirements)
      • Implement infection control protocols compliant with AS/NZS 4187 and current cosmetic medicine guidelines
      • Source and maintain required clinical equipment: treatment chairs, medication refrigeration, sharps disposal, and resuscitation equipment
      • Obtain council and local government approval for operating a medical or healthcare facility at your chosen premises
      Key Considerations
      • Emergency management kit including adrenaline (epinephrine) must be accessible at every treatment session — non-negotiable
      • Some states require facility inspection and ongoing registration renewal — budget time and cost for compliance
      • Co-locating within an existing medical centre can significantly reduce compliance burden and initial capital outlay
      • Home-based clinics are subject to council zoning regulations and may face restrictions — verify before committing
      • Premises insurance is separate from professional indemnity — you need both; your landlord's insurance does not cover clinical activities
      State requirements differ significantly. Queensland and New South Wales have the most prescriptive facility standards. Check your state health department before signing a lease.

      STEP 4

      Insurance & Legal Structure

      Required
      What This Involves
      • Obtain professional indemnity insurance that explicitly covers cosmetic injectable procedures — general PI policies often exclude cosmetic work
      • Set up an appropriate business structure: sole trader, company, or trust — each has different tax, liability, and growth implications
      • Register your ABN and business name with ASIC and the Australian Business Register before commencing trade
      • Create legally compliant patient consent forms and comprehensive treatment documentation — these are your primary liability protection
      • Consider employment agreements or contractor arrangements for any staff, using healthcare-specific templates
      Key Considerations
      • PI insurance must specifically cover cosmetic injectables — minimum $20M aggregate coverage is standard for cosmetic medicine practitioners
      • Specialist cosmetic medicine insurers (e.g. MDA National, AVANT, Guild) provide more relevant coverage than general business insurers
      • Company structure provides stronger personal asset protection but adds ASIC reporting and company tax obligations
      • Patient consent forms should be drafted or reviewed by a health law solicitor — generic templates may not meet current legal standards
      • Public liability insurance (clinic space) is separate from professional indemnity — you need both types of coverage
      Do not skip legal review. Inadequate consent forms or incorrect business structure are among the most common and costly mistakes for new clinic owners.
      STEP 5

      Marketing & Advertising Compliance

      Ongoing Responsibility
      What This Involves
      • Understand the TGA Therapeutic Goods Advertising Code — applies to any advertising of scheduled medicines or medical devices to consumers
      • Review AHPRA's advertising guidelines for registered health services including social media, websites, and all digital and print content
      • Conduct a full compliance audit of all social media profiles, website content, and promotional materials before launch
      • Develop internal processes and staff training for creating compliant marketing content on an ongoing basis
      • Understand obligations around patient reviews, referral arrangements, and promotional partnerships
      Key Considerations
      • Before/after photographs require informed consent, specific disclosures, and must not imply unrealistic outcomes
      • Testimonials referencing a registered health practitioner's services are prohibited under AHPRA advertising guidelines
      • TGA prohibits advertising prescription medicines (anti-wrinkle injectables) by brand or generic name to the general public
      • Promotional pricing requires careful wording to avoid implying reduced safety or quality standards
      • Non-compliance can result in formal complaints, mandatory corrective action, fines, and AHPRA registration proceedings
      Ongoing responsibility. Advertising compliance requires continuous review as social media platforms evolve and regulatory interpretations are updated by AHPRA and TGA.
      STEP 6

      Ongoing CPD & Continuous Learning

      Continuous Learning
      What This Involves
      • Meet annual AHPRA CPD requirements — typically 20 hours for RNs/ENs and 35+ hours for medical practitioners per registration year
      • Include cosmetic-specific CPD activities each year — general clinical CPD does not fully substitute for specialty-relevant learning
      • Maintain emergency management competency including anaphylaxis and vascular occlusion protocols — refresh at least annually
      • Attend industry conferences and training events: ASAM, ACAM, and AIAM annual meetings offer high-quality CPD and peer learning
      • Build a documented Professional Development Plan aligned with your clinical goals for AHPRA compliance and career progression
      Key Considerations
      • CPD must be relevant to your scope of practice and properly documented — vague records are insufficient for AHPRA compliance audits
      • Vascular occlusion and anaphylaxis management training should be refreshed annually given the high-stakes nature of these emergencies
      • Online CPD must be genuinely educational and verifiable — random online courses may not meet AHPRA quality standards
      • Professional association membership (ASAM, ACAM, AIAM) typically includes CPD opportunities, peer networks, and advocacy resources
      • Advanced training in cannula technique, facial anatomy, and new product indications can expand your clinical scope and market positioning
      Learning never stops. The cosmetic injectable industry evolves rapidly — practitioners who invest in ongoing education consistently achieve better clinical outcomes and long-term business success.

      State & Territory Considerations

      While core national regulations apply, each state and territory has its own health laws, facility requirements, and notification processes. Always verify current requirements with the relevant authority.

      • AHPRA registration & Board endorsements required

      • Notify your local Public Health Unit

      • Comply with WA Health facility guidelines
      • Local government approval required
      • Notify Department of Health
